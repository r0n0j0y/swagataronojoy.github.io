অনুষ্ঠান (Ceremony)

Laced with elaborate rituals and colorful customs, বিয়ে (biye) - a Bengali wedding, is an occasion of great revelry and jolly celebrations. Listed below are some interesting and important Bengali wedding rituals that form an integral part of our marriage.

মঙ্গল ঘট (Mangal Ghat)

Auspicious Vessel

Other than colourful rangolis done by the women, 'mangal ghats' or clay vessels that hold mango leaves and a coconut on top, with a symbol similar to the swastika in vermilion, are placed in the four corners of the house. These vessels are used to ward off spirits and usher in good fortune, in unison with a garland of mango leaves. The entrance of the bride's home usually sports a copper version of the mangal ghat.

আইবুরো ভাত (Ai Buro Bhat)

Last Meal

This happens before the actual wedding ceremony (or a few days before) where the bride and groom take their last meals as unmarried people.

আলতা (Aalta)

Lac-dye

Instead of mehndi, brides apply a red pigment called 'aalta' on their hands and the soles of their feet, as a symbol of Goddess Lakshmi who will bless her husband's home with wealth and prosperity.

আশীর্বাদ (Ashirbaad)

Blessings

On an auspicious day the elders of the groom's side go to bless the bride and vice versa, by sprinkling husked rice and trefoil on their heads and giving them gold ornaments. It is a kind of acceptance of the boy and the girl on both sides.

দধি মঙ্গল (Dodhi Mangal)

Auspicious Yogurt

At dawn on the day of marriage seven married ladies adorn the bride's hands with the traditional bangles Shakha and Paula – one pair of red and one pair of white bangles, and feed her a meal of yogurt (curd) and rice, the only meal after which the bride and her parents fast the whole day.

গায়ে হলুদ (Gaaye Holud)

Turmeric Ceremony

Here, five to seven married women of the household grind turmeric with mortar and pestle and anoint the bride with turmeric paste. The bride is made to wear Shakha Pola (special bangles made of shell and coral). Gaye Holud is also celebrated on groom's side. This marks the start of their readiness for the wedding for both bride and groom.

তত্ত্ব (Tottwo)

Basic Gifts

Tottwo tends to be identified as starter items for the couple as they begin their new married life. After the gaaye holud, one set is for the bride from the groom's house. The gifts include sarees, and cosmetics to go with them. Also among the gifts are fish, assorted sweets, curd, paan, dhaan and durba. A relative of the groom arrives at the bride's house with an entourage of helpers bearing the gifts. Incense is lit welcome them and conch shells are blown. The bearers are given sweets. Reciprocally, gifts also come from the bride's house. It includes a saree for the groom's mother, and fish, sweets, curd, paan, dhaan, and durba. The gifts come on a brass plate or kasar thala borne by helpers from the bride's house. They are welcomed as warmly as the groom's gift bearers who visit the bride's house.

বরযাত্রী (Bor Jatri)

The Groom's Journey

The members of the groom's house as well as his friends journey to the bride's house where the wedding takes place.

বরবরণ (Bor Boron)

The Groom's Welcome

When the bor jatri reaches the bride's place, usually the mother of the bride along with other members from the bride's side come out to welcome the groom and his family by showing the holy earthen lamp, sprinkling trefoil, and husked rice placed on a bamboo winnow (kula). Then they are served sweets and drinks.

উলুধ্বনিি (Ullu Dhwoni)

Ululating

Hulhuli is the Bengali custom of ululating. While most believe it is done to ward off evil spirits, it is also done as a signal for the beginning/end of important occasions and events. Hulhuli is usually accompanied by the blowing of the conch shell (shankh).

পত্র বস্ত্র (Potto Bostro)

New Clothes

After the groom is seated at the chadnatolla (wedding altar and canopy) – the sanctum sanctorum where only the groom, bride and the priest takes their place, the groom is offered new clothes by the person who is to do the sampradaan – the elderly male member of the family who does sampradan offers the responsibility of the bride to the groom.

সাত পাক (Shaat Paak)

Seven Rounds

The bride sits on a wooden stool called a pidi/piri, and is carried to the mandap by her brothers or uncles. The bride is not supposed to see her groom when she enters the mandap, so she has to keep her eyes covered with sacred beetle leaves. Keeping her lifted up on the stool, her brothers’ then walk around the groom seven times.

শুভদৃষ্টি (Subho Drishti)

First Sighting

After saat paak, the bride and the groom are made to look at each other in front of all the assembled invitees. The bride is told to remove the paan (beetul) leaves. This exchange of loving glance is to initiate them to be together officially by the society.

মালাবদল (Mala Bodol)

Exchanging Garlands

After the circles are completed, still sitting high on the piri, the bride and the groom exchange garlands of fragrant flowers thrice. This is the first step in which they accept each other.

সম্প্রদান (Sampradan)

Bestowing

After mala bodol, the bride then takes her place at the chadnatolla where an elderly male member of the bride's family hands her over to the groom and the couple's hands are bound by the sacred thread amidst recital of Vedic chants and are placed on the mangal ghot.

যজ্ঞ (Yagna)

Holy Fire

The bride and groom sit in front of the sacred fire and chant mantras after the priest. Lord Agni, the fire God is made the divine witness to the marriage.

সপ্তপদী (Saptapadi)

Seven Rounds

Seven circular rounds are taken by the couple around the fire thereby solemnising the occasion. The bride and the groom also have to touch seven suparis kept on seven paan leaves with their toes.

অঞ্জলি (Anjali)

Offering

An offering to the fire is made. The bride's brother puts puffed rice (khoi) in the hands of the bride, and the groom standing close to her holds her hands from the back and extends their arms forward. They then pour the offering into the fire together.

সিঁদুর দান (Sindoor Daan)

Vermillion

Once again seated at their respective places in chadnatolla the groom applies sindoor or vermilion (a symbol of marriage worn by Hindu women thereafter) on the bride's hair-parting. This formalizes them as husband and wife.

ঘোমটা (Ghomta)

Veil

Seated at their respective places in chadnatolla the newly designated bride then covers her head with a new sari offered by the groom as ghomta or veil.

বাসর ঘর (Bashor Ghar)

Bridal Chamber

After the ceremony, the groom is expected to stay the night at the wedding venue or the bride's home. A group of cousins and friends from both sides keep the couple awake and entertained all night with jokes, anecdotes and chit chat.

বিদায় (Bidaay)

Farewell

This is a mixed moment of joy and sorrow as the bride is bid adieu with blessings of her parents and relatives to start a new life with her beau. The bride has to throw rice in the sari of her mother to fill the responsibility of her mother to her.

বধূবরণ (Bodhu Boron)

Bride's Welcome

This is done when the bride reaches the groom's house. One dish is made full with alta and milk. The bride stands on it, then enters the room with groom after being welcomed by the mother-in-law.

কালরাত্রি (Kaal Ratri)

Night Apart

After the couple reaches the groom's house and the initial welcome ceremony is over they are separated for the night, probably to get a refreshing sleep and prepare for the next day's final wedding ceremony.

বউ ভাত (Bou Bhaat)

Bride's Feast

The bride cooks and serves all the members of her husband's family. The husband has to gift a sari to new bride and has to take a vow of taking the responsibility of the bride's basic needs ("Bhaat Kapor" - literally meaning food and dress). A banquet is held to treat the guests who lavish gifts on the new bride.
